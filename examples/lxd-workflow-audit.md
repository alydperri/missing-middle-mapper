# Workflow Audit: Instructional Design Project Workflow

## Workflow-level framing

**Workflow name:** Instructional Design Project Workflow

**Outcome:** Produce learning assets that measurably close a defined performance or knowledge gap for a specific audience, delivered on time and within agreed scope.

**Frequency:** 8-12 projects per quarter across the team (4 designers). Each project runs 3-8 weeks depending on scope.

**Owner:** Learning & Talent Development team.

**What "good" looks like:** A project runs well when the problem is correctly diagnosed during intake (not just accepted at face value), the design matches the actual learner need (not just the stakeholder request), the content is produced without unnecessary rework, revision cycles are focused and finite, and the launched asset produces a measurable change in the target behavior or knowledge area.

---

## Workflow value

**What happens if this workflow breaks down or runs badly?** Employees get training that doesn't address the actual problem. Time and budget are spent building the wrong thing. Stakeholders lose trust in the L&D team, which makes future investment harder to secure. At worst, a critical skill or compliance gap goes unaddressed because the training was misdiagnosed. The downstream cost is real: onboarding takes longer, tools are adopted more slowly, managers don't get the support they need for their teams.

**How many people are affected?** Directly, every new hire goes through L&D-produced onboarding. Ongoing, the team produces learning assets for ~2,000 employees across all functions. Some assets (manager training, compliance, product knowledge) touch hundreds of people per cycle. The team also supports customer education indirectly through enablement content.

**How much time does this workflow consume?** Each project runs 3-8 weeks. At any given time, 4 designers are running 2-3 active projects each. Rough estimate: 60-80 designer-hours per project across all steps, plus 10-20 hours of stakeholder and SME time. At 8-12 projects per quarter, that's 500-1,000 hours of L&D capacity per quarter on project work alone.

**Is this connected to something leadership cares about?** Yes. HubSpot's internal AI transformation requires significant upskilling and behavior change, which runs through L&D. The team is also expected to model AI-enabled ways of working, which makes the meta-question (can L&D redesign its own workflow with AI?) strategically visible.

**If AI made this significantly better, would the people doing the work feel it?** Yes. The friction data is clear: designers spend the majority of their time on production work (Step 4) and revision management (Step 5), which are the steps with the most automation and augmentation potential. Reducing time on those steps would free up capacity for the work designers find most meaningful — diagnosis, design, and judgment. The delight potential is high.

## Step 1: Intake & Request Triage

**What triggers it:** A stakeholder submits a request for training, onboarding content, or a learning resource. Requests come through email, Slack, or a shared intake form (inconsistently used).

**What it produces:** A decision to proceed, push back, redirect, or request more information. If proceeding, an initial scope agreement (usually verbal or in a brief Slack thread).

### Judgment layer

**What would a new person get wrong?**
They would accept the request as stated. Experienced designers know that the stated need is often not the real need. A request for "a training on X" is frequently a symptom of a process problem, a communication gap, or a change management issue that training won't solve. The first judgment call is whether training is even the right intervention.

**What information is used that isn't written down?**
Stakeholder history. Who is asking matters: some stakeholders have a track record of clear, well-scoped requests. Others consistently underestimate scope or change direction mid-project. Experienced designers adjust their intake process based on who's across the table. They also factor in what else is on the team's plate, which isn't visible in the request.

**What causes breakdowns?**
Requests accepted without enough diagnostic questioning. The designer says yes too early, scope is agreed verbally, and then the project expands without a clear anchor point to push back against. The other common breakdown: the intake form isn't used consistently, so critical context is scattered across Slack threads and emails instead of captured in one place.

**Who else is involved?**
The requesting stakeholder, sometimes their manager. Occasionally a subject matter expert is referenced but not yet engaged.

### Classification signals

**Checklist or judgment?** Split. The mechanics of receiving and logging a request are procedural. The decision about whether to proceed, push back, or redirect requires significant judgment.

**Output consistency?** High variance. Some designers run a structured diagnostic intake. Others accept requests with minimal questioning. The quality of the intake directly affects everything downstream.

**Error cost?** Medium-high. A badly scoped intake leads to rework in design and development. The cost is time, not catastrophic failure, but it compounds.

**Bottleneck?** Moderate. Intake isn't the slowest step, but a scattered intake process means information has to be re-gathered later.

**Friction:** Logging requests and chasing down basic information (who's the audience, what's the timeline, what's been tried before) is tedious and repetitive. The diagnostic conversation itself is energizing — that's where the designer adds real value.

### Current AI usage

Not currently using AI in this step. Intake is handled manually through email, Slack, and the intake form.

## Step 2: Discovery & Needs Analysis

**What triggers it:** An accepted request with enough initial context to begin discovery. Sometimes triggered by the designer realizing during intake that more investigation is needed before committing to scope.

**What it produces:** A diagnosis of the actual gap (performance, knowledge, process, or motivation), a set of documented findings, and enough context to make informed design decisions. May also produce a recommendation to not build training if the root cause is something else.

### Judgment layer

**What would a new person get wrong?**
They would ask surface-level questions and take the answers at face value. Experienced designers know how to probe: when a stakeholder says "people don't know how to do X," the experienced designer asks "how do you know?" and "what happens when they try?" The real gap is often different from the stated one. A new person would also stop discovery too early or go too deep — calibrating the right depth is a learned skill.

**What information is used that isn't written down?**
Pattern recognition from previous projects. An experienced designer recognizes when a "training problem" is actually a process problem, a tooling problem, or a management problem. They also know which questions to ask in which order — and which to hold back for later. Institutional memory about what's been tried before and what failed.

**What causes breakdowns?**
Discovery depth varies by designer, not by project need. Some designers do thorough needs analysis for every project; others barely skim. Interview notes are captured in whatever format the individual prefers — rarely in a reusable or searchable way. Findings from discovery almost never surface back to the team for pattern recognition across projects.

**Who else is involved?**
Stakeholders, subject matter experts, sometimes end-users. The quality of discovery depends heavily on the designer's ability to build enough trust quickly to get candid answers.

### Classification signals

**Checklist or judgment?** Primarily judgment. You can provide a question bank, but knowing which questions to ask, when to probe deeper, and when to pivot is judgment.

**Output consistency?** Low. Discovery output varies dramatically by designer. One designer produces a tight diagnostic brief; another produces scattered notes.

**Error cost?** High. A misdiagnosed need leads to a well-built solution for the wrong problem. The error often isn't discovered until post-launch.

**Bottleneck?** Sometimes. Discovery can stall when stakeholders are slow to schedule interviews or provide access.

**Friction:** Scheduling interviews and chasing people down is tedious. Synthesizing notes from multiple conversations into a coherent finding is cognitively heavy — the designer is holding a lot of context from different sources and looking for patterns. The diagnostic conversation itself is the most rewarding part.

### Current AI usage

One designer on the team has started using ChatGPT to summarize interview notes after discovery calls. She pastes her rough notes in and asks for a structured summary. She says it saves about 15 minutes per call, but she still has to check the output because it sometimes invents details that weren't discussed or misses nuance in what the stakeholder said. The rest of the team isn't doing this. No shared practice or standard.

## Step 3: Instructional Design

**What triggers it:** Completed discovery with a clear diagnosis of the gap and enough context to make design decisions.

**What it produces:** A design document (or equivalent) that specifies learning objectives, structure, modality, assessment approach, and the rationale behind key design decisions.

### Judgment layer

**What would a new person get wrong?**
They would default to familiar modalities instead of matching modality to audience and context. "Let's build an e-learning" when the real need is a job aid. "Let's do a live workshop" when the audience is distributed and asynchronous. They would also include too much — experienced designers know that what you leave out is often more important than what you include. Deciding the right scope and depth for a specific audience is a taste call.

**What information is used that isn't written down?**
The team's implicit quality standards. What does "good" look like for a particular type of learning asset? What level of interactivity is worth the development cost? When is a simple document better than a produced module? These standards exist in the heads of experienced designers but aren't codified. Also, the balance between what the stakeholder wants and what the learner needs — experienced designers know when to push back on stakeholder preferences that would hurt the learning experience.

**What causes breakdowns?**
Design rationale is rarely documented. The designer makes decisions based on experience, but the reasoning lives only in their head. When the design is reviewed, stakeholders question choices and the designer has to reconstruct the rationale. If the designer is unavailable, no one else can explain why the design looks the way it does. Inconsistent quality standards across the team mean two designers might produce very different designs for similar briefs.

**Who else is involved?**
The stakeholder reviews and approves the design direction. Sometimes a peer designer provides input. Subject matter experts may validate content accuracy.

### Classification signals

**Checklist or judgment?** Primarily judgment. There are frameworks (ADDIE, SAM, etc.) but applying them well is a judgment call. The design decisions that matter most are about emphasis, structure, and "what does good look like here" — not procedural.

**Output consistency?** Moderate. The format is somewhat consistent (design docs follow a loose template), but the quality and depth of design thinking varies across the team.

**Error cost?** Medium-high. A weak design leads to a polished asset that doesn't actually teach anything. The cost is development time spent building the wrong thing.

**Bottleneck?** Not usually. This step moves at the pace of the designer.

**Friction:** Generating learning objectives from discovery notes is formulaic and tedious — it could follow a pattern. Scaffolding the structure from a template is mechanical. But the taste calls — modality selection, scope, emphasis, what to cut — are where the designer does their best work and finds the most meaning.

### Current AI usage

Not currently using AI in this step. Design decisions are made manually based on discovery findings and experience.

## Step 4: Content Development

**What triggers it:** An approved design document (or at least a verbal agreement on design direction, which is more common than it should be).

**What it produces:** The actual learning asset: e-learning module, facilitator guide, job aid, video script, or whatever the design specified.

### Judgment layer

**What would a new person get wrong?**
They would start from scratch every time. Experienced designers know what reusable components exist, where to find previous work that can be adapted, and how to repurpose existing content rather than rebuilding. They also know when a first draft is "good enough" to share versus when it needs another pass — that calibration saves significant time. A new person would also miss tone and voice consistency, especially across a multi-part program.

**What information is used that isn't written down?**
The team's implicit voice and tone standards. What does HubSpot learning content sound like? There's no documented style guide specific to L&D. Individual designers have internalized a voice, but it varies across the team. Also: knowledge of what existing assets could be repurposed, which lives in individual memory, not a searchable repository.

**What causes breakdowns?**
This is where the most time is spent, and it's the most variable step. Content is rebuilt from scratch when similar assets already exist because there's no good way to find or adapt them. Inconsistent voice and tone across the team means content from different designers feels like it comes from different organizations. The gap between a first draft and stakeholder expectations is often larger than it needs to be because quality standards aren't externalized.

**Who else is involved?**
Subject matter experts for content validation. Sometimes a multimedia producer for video or interactive elements. Stakeholders see drafts during this step.

### Classification signals

**Checklist or judgment?** Split. Much of the production work (formatting, templating, building in the authoring tool) is procedural. The quality calls while drafting (is this clear? is this the right level of detail? does this match the learning objectives?) are judgment.

**Output consistency?** Low-to-moderate. The quality of the final asset depends heavily on individual designer skill, available time, and whether the design was thorough.

**Error cost?** Low-to-medium per individual decision. Errors in content can be caught in review. The bigger risk is cumulative: inconsistent quality erodes trust in the L&D team over time.

**Bottleneck?** Yes. This is consistently the longest step and the one most affected by competing priorities and resource constraints.

**Friction:** Formatting, templating, and basic production work is tedious and time-consuming. Rebuilding content from scratch when something similar exists somewhere is deeply frustrating. The actual writing and crafting — translating the design into clear, engaging content — is where the designer's skill shows and is generally satisfying.

### Current AI usage

Two of the four designers use ChatGPT for first-draft generation on content development. They paste the design doc (or a summary of it) and ask for a draft of the learning content. Both say it speeds up the initial drafting by 30-40%. One says she edits heavily — "it gets the structure right but the tone is off, it sounds like a textbook." The other admits he edits less than he probably should — "I used to rewrite most of it, but honestly the drafts have gotten good enough that I mostly just tweak." Neither has a formal review checklist. The other two designers don't use AI for content development at all. There's no team standard.

## Step 5: Stakeholder Review & Revision

**What triggers it:** A draft (or iteration) of the learning asset is ready for review.

**What it produces:** Feedback from stakeholders, consolidated and incorporated (or pushed back on), leading to a final version ready for launch.

### Judgment layer

**What would a new person get wrong?**
They would incorporate all feedback uncritically. Experienced designers know which feedback to take, which to push back on, and which to acknowledge but deprioritize. They also know how to manage stakeholders who try to reopen design decisions during content review — a common pattern that can derail timelines. Knowing when revision cycles are producing diminishing returns (the changes are getting smaller but the rounds keep happening) is a critical calibration.

**What information is used that isn't written down?**
The political dynamics of the stakeholder group. Who has final authority? Whose feedback is actually mandatory versus whose is advisory? What are the stakeholder's unstated preferences that drive their feedback? Experienced designers read these dynamics and manage the review process accordingly.

**What causes breakdowns?**
Revision rounds are open-ended. There's no agreed stopping criteria — no definition of "done" for review. Feedback arrives in multiple formats (email, Slack, comments in the doc, verbal in a meeting) and is not consolidated or tracked. The same comments surface repeatedly because issues weren't caught earlier in the process — often because the design review was insufficient or skipped.

**Who else is involved?**
Primary stakeholder, sometimes their manager, subject matter experts, legal or compliance in regulated content areas. The number of reviewers often expands beyond what was agreed at intake.

### Classification signals

**Checklist or judgment?** Primarily judgment for feedback triage. The mechanics of tracking changes and managing versions are procedural.

**Output consistency?** Low. Some projects go through two rounds of focused review. Others go through five or six rounds of unfocused back-and-forth.

**Error cost?** Medium. Incorporating bad feedback degrades the learning experience. Pushing back too aggressively damages the stakeholder relationship.

**Bottleneck?** Yes. Review cycles are one of the most common reasons projects run over timeline.

**Friction:** Consolidating feedback from multiple sources in multiple formats is tedious and error-prone. Managing stakeholders who want to relitigate design decisions is draining. The judgment work — deciding what feedback improves the asset and what feedback would make it worse — is meaningful but politically delicate.

### Current AI usage

Not currently using AI in this step. Feedback consolidation and stakeholder management are handled manually.

## Step 6: Launch & Measurement

**What triggers it:** Final stakeholder sign-off on the learning asset.

**What it produces:** A deployed learning asset accessible to the target audience, with (ideally) tracking in place to measure completion and impact.

### Judgment layer

**What would a new person get wrong?**
They would treat launch as the finish line. Experienced designers know that measurement is where you learn whether the project actually worked — but measurement is also where most projects fall short. A new person would default to tracking completion rates (did people finish the course?) rather than impact (did behavior change?). They also wouldn't know when to iterate on a launched asset versus when to retire it.

**What information is used that isn't written down?**
What "success" actually looks like for different types of learning interventions. Completion rate is the default metric because it's easy to capture, but it tells you almost nothing about impact. Connecting learning activity to business outcomes requires knowing what to measure, how to set up the measurement, and how to have credible conversations with stakeholders about what the data means. That expertise is rare on the team and entirely undocumented.

**What causes breakdowns?**
Measurement defaults to completion rates because that's what the LMS tracks out of the box. Baselines are almost never established before launch, making "improvement" impossible to prove. Assets are launched and never revisited — there's no systematic process for reviewing performance data and deciding whether to iterate or retire. When measurement does happen, findings rarely feed back into the intake process for future projects.

**Who else is involved?**
The LMS administrator for deployment. The stakeholder for communication to the target audience. Analytics or People Ops if deeper measurement is attempted.

### Classification signals

**Checklist or judgment?** Split. Deployment, tracking setup, and data collection are procedural. Deciding what to measure, interpreting results in context, and deciding whether to iterate or retire require judgment.

**Output consistency?** Low. Some projects get robust measurement plans. Most get completion tracking and nothing else.

**Error cost?** The direct error cost is low (a bad measurement plan doesn't hurt the learner). The organizational cost is high: without measurement, the L&D team can't prove impact, which undermines credibility and investment over time.

**Bottleneck?** Not a time bottleneck, but a capability bottleneck — the team doesn't consistently have the skills or systems to measure beyond completion.

**Friction:** Setting up tracking and pulling completion data is tedious but straightforward. The painful part is knowing that completion data doesn't actually tell you whether the training worked, and not having a good alternative. Interpreting results and having the "did this work?" conversation with stakeholders is meaningful but uncommon.

### Current AI usage

Not currently using AI in this step. Measurement is done through the LMS and manual data pulls.
