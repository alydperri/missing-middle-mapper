# Workflow Audit: Internal Content Approval Process

## Workflow-level framing

**Workflow name:** Internal Content Approval Process

**Outcome:** Ensure all externally published content (blog posts, social media, customer emails, partner communications) meets brand standards, legal requirements, and strategic messaging guidelines before publication.

**Frequency:** 30-50 pieces per week across the team. Volume spikes around product launches and campaigns.

**Owner:** Corporate Communications team (6 people: 2 writers, 1 editor, 1 social media manager, 1 comms lead, 1 director).

**What "good" looks like:** Content moves from draft to published within 48 hours for standard pieces, 24 hours for time-sensitive pieces. Every published piece is on-brand, factually accurate, legally safe, and aligned with current messaging priorities. No published content requires post-publication correction.

---

## Workflow value

**What happens if this workflow breaks down or runs badly?** Off-brand or factually incorrect content reaches customers and the public. Legal risk if compliance-sensitive content isn't reviewed. Messaging contradicts product positioning or executive communications. Reputational damage scales with the channel: a bad social post is embarrassing, a bad customer email can cost deals, a bad press statement can become a news cycle.

**How many people are affected?** The content reaches external audiences across blog, social, email, and partner channels — collectively tens of thousands of readers per week. Internally, 15-20 content creators across functions submit into this workflow. The 6-person comms team processes everything.

**How much time does this workflow consume?** At 30-50 pieces per week, the editor spends roughly 20-25 hours per week on review alone. Add revision cycles, approval chasing, and publication: the comms team estimates 50-60% of their collective capacity goes to this workflow. During launch weeks, it's higher.

**Is this connected to something leadership cares about?** Indirectly. Brand consistency and speed-to-publish have been raised in quarterly reviews. No dedicated initiative, but the pain is visible to leadership when time-sensitive content misses its window.

**If AI made this significantly better, would the people doing the work feel it?** The editor and comms lead would feel it most. The editorial review and approval steps are the biggest bottlenecks and the most frustrating parts of the workflow. Reducing review time and clarifying approval criteria would free up significant capacity. Content creators across the company would feel it too — faster turnaround on their submissions.

---

## Step 1: Content Submission

**What triggers it:** A content creator (internal or cross-functional) completes a draft and submits it for review. Submissions come through a shared Google Drive folder with a standardized naming convention and a brief intake form.

**What it produces:** A queued draft with metadata: author, content type, target channel, requested publish date, audience, and any flagged sensitivities (legal, competitive, executive-adjacent).

### Judgment layer

**What would a new person get wrong?**
Not much. The submission process is well-documented. The intake form captures the necessary metadata. A new person might miscategorize the content type or miss a sensitivity flag, but the form prompts for these.

**What information is used that isn't written down?**
The naming convention and folder structure are documented. The only undocumented piece: some content creators informally Slack the comms lead to flag urgency before submitting, which effectively jumps the queue. This isn't a formal process but it happens 2-3 times per week.

**What causes breakdowns?**
Occasionally a submission arrives without the intake form completed, which means the reviewer has to chase down metadata before starting. Cross-functional submitters (product marketing, sales enablement) sometimes bypass the Google Drive folder entirely and send drafts via email or Slack, which breaks the tracking.

**Who else is involved?**
The content creator (submitter). The comms lead informally for queue-jumping urgent requests.

### Classification signals

**Checklist or judgment?** Mostly checklist. The submission process is standardized.

**Output consistency?** High when the process is followed. Medium when submissions bypass the standard channel.

**Error cost?** Low. A bad submission just delays the process.

**Bottleneck?** No. Submission is fast.

**Friction:** Chasing down missing metadata is annoying but infrequent. The process itself is fine.

### Current AI usage

Not currently using AI in this step.

---

## Step 2: Editorial Review

**What triggers it:** A submitted draft appears in the review queue.

**What it produces:** An edited draft with tracked changes, or a request for revisions sent back to the author.

### Judgment layer

**What would a new person get wrong?**
It depends on the situation. Different content types need different levels of review. A blog post gets a different treatment than a customer email. But the specific criteria for each... that's hard to pin down.

**What information is used that isn't written down?**
The editor has a sense of what "sounds right" for the brand. There's a style guide, but it doesn't cover everything. A lot of the editorial judgment is about tone, emphasis, and knowing what the exec team cares about this week.

**What causes breakdowns?**
Sometimes things get sent back multiple times. The feedback isn't always clear on what needs to change.

**Who else is involved?**
The editor, sometimes the comms lead for sensitive pieces.

### Classification signals

**Checklist or judgment?** Judgment, mostly.

**Output consistency?** It depends on who's reviewing.

**Error cost?** I mean, if something goes out with errors, that's bad. But the review usually catches things.

**Bottleneck?** This is where things slow down, especially during launches.

**Friction:** It can be a lot, especially during peak times.

### Current AI usage

The editor doesn't use AI for review. A couple of the content creators who submit drafts mention using ChatGPT or Grammarly to polish their drafts before submitting, but that's on the submission side, not the review side.

---

## Step 3: Revision & Resubmission

**What triggers it:** The editor sends the draft back to the author with requested changes.

**What it produces:** A revised draft resubmitted for another review pass.

### Judgment layer

**What would a new person get wrong?**
Not much on the author side. They make the requested changes and resubmit. The judgment is really on the editor side: did the revision address the feedback?

**What information is used that isn't written down?**
Nothing significant on the revision step itself. The judgment lives in the review step.

**What causes breakdowns?**
Revision cycles can multiply. The author makes changes, the editor finds new issues in the revision (or the comms lead weighs in for the first time and introduces new feedback), and the cycle continues. There's no agreed limit on revision rounds.

**Who else is involved?**
The author and the editor. Sometimes a subject matter expert if factual accuracy is in question.

### Classification signals

**Checklist or judgment?** Mostly procedural. The author follows the editor's tracked changes.

**Output consistency?** Depends on the clarity of the editorial feedback. Vague feedback leads to revisions that miss the point.

**Error cost?** Low per round. The cumulative cost is time: extra rounds delay publication.

**Bottleneck?** Yes, when revision cycles multiply.

**Friction:** Going back and forth when the feedback isn't clear is frustrating. Especially when new reviewers show up in round 2 or 3 with feedback that could have been given earlier.

### Current AI usage

Some writers use ChatGPT to help rewrite sections based on editorial feedback. They paste the feedback and the original text and ask for a revised version. No one tracks whether this is happening or checks the quality of AI-revised content specifically.

---

## Step 4: Approval

**What triggers it:** The editor considers the draft ready for final sign-off.

**What it produces:** Approved content cleared for publication.

### Judgment layer

**What would a new person get wrong?**
They wouldn't know who actually needs to approve what. Different content types apparently require different approvers, but the rules aren't documented.

**What information is used that isn't written down?**
Who has approval authority for what content type. Whether legal needs to see it. Whether the director needs to see it or if the comms lead can approve. This seems to change depending on the topic and the political sensitivity.

**What causes breakdowns?**
Content sits waiting for approval because it's not clear whose inbox it's in. Sometimes the approver is the comms lead, sometimes the director, sometimes both. For legally sensitive content, legal review adds time but the criteria for "legally sensitive" aren't defined.

**Who else is involved?**
The comms lead, the director, sometimes legal. Who's involved seems to depend on the content.

### Classification signals

**Checklist or judgment?** It should be a checklist but it isn't. There's no documented approval criteria.

**Output consistency?** Low. Same content type gets different levels of scrutiny depending on who's approving and what week it is.

**Error cost?** Potentially high. If something is approved that shouldn't have been (legal risk, off-brand messaging, factually incorrect), the damage is to the company's reputation.

**Bottleneck?** Yes. Approval delays are the number one complaint from content creators.

**Friction:** Figuring out who needs to approve something and then waiting for them to do it. This is the most painful step in the workflow.

### Current AI usage

Not currently using AI in this step. Approval is entirely manual.

---

## Step 5: Publication

**What triggers it:** Final approval received.

**What it produces:** Published content on the target channel (blog, social, email platform, partner portal).

### Judgment layer

**What would a new person get wrong?**
Formatting for the specific channel. Each channel has different requirements (character limits, image specs, CTA placement). There's a checklist for this, though it's slightly out of date.

**What information is used that isn't written down?**
Optimal posting times for different channels and audiences. The social media manager has this dialed but it's in her head.

**What causes breakdowns?**
Rarely. Publication is the smoothest step. Occasional formatting errors when content is moved between platforms. Sometimes the wrong version gets published (pre-approval draft instead of final), which is a versioning issue, not a judgment issue.

**Who else is involved?**
The social media manager for social channels. The writer or comms lead for blog and email.

### Classification signals

**Checklist or judgment?** Mostly checklist. Channel formatting requirements are documented (if slightly outdated).

**Output consistency?** High. Once content is approved, publication is reliable.

**Error cost?** Low-to-medium. A formatting error is embarrassing but fixable. Publishing the wrong version is more damaging but rare.

**Bottleneck?** No. This step is fast.

**Friction:** Reformatting the same content for multiple channels is tedious. Copy-pasting between platforms and adjusting formatting each time.

### Current AI usage

The social media manager uses an AI feature in the scheduling tool to suggest optimal posting times. She says it's "mostly right" and she overrides it about 20% of the time based on her own sense of when the audience is active. For multi-channel reformatting, no AI is used — it's manual copy-paste.
