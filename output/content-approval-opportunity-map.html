01
Content Submission
Creator submits draft via Google Drive with intake form metadata
Automate Stop / Rethink
+
Hidden judgment layer

What actually happens here

  • Creator completes a standardized intake form (content type, channel, publish date, sensitivity flags) and drops the draft into a shared Google Drive folder
  • The process is well-documented; a new person would get it right if they follow the form
  • Informally, some creators Slack the comms lead to flag urgency before submitting — this effectively jumps the queue 2–3 times per week without being a formal process

Where it breaks down

  • Submissions arrive without the intake form completed, requiring the reviewer to chase metadata before review can start
  • Cross-functional submitters (product marketing, sales enablement) bypass Google Drive entirely — sending via email or Slack — which breaks tracking
  • The informal urgency Slack creates a shadow queue that isn't visible to everyone and has no documented criteria
Automate
Intake validation and queue entry: automatically check submitted drafts for form completeness before they enter the review queue. Flag missing fields and bounce back to the submitter with a clear request — no reviewer time spent chasing metadata. Also: auto-confirm receipt, log to queue with metadata, and surface estimated review window based on current load.
⚠ Watch for
Brittleness: this automation depends on a consistent submission channel (Google Drive + intake form). It will silently fail when submitters bypass the channel via email or Slack. The automation cannot catch what it never sees. Fix the channel problem first — or build a parallel intake bot that can accept submissions from multiple surfaces.
Stop / Rethink
The informal Slack queue-jump: 2–3 times per week, urgency is communicated through a back-channel that has no documented criteria and isn't visible to the full team. This is not a submission variant — it's a process hole. Automating intake validation without addressing this would create a clean formal queue running parallel to an unaccountable informal one. The informal practice should be replaced with a documented urgency tier in the intake form before any automation is applied to prioritization.
⚠ Watch for
Automation trap: do not build priority routing logic into the intake automation until the urgency criteria are documented. Automating undocumented priority rules would encode the current informal practice into the system — harder to change later and invisible to anyone who didn't know about the original back-channel.
02
Editorial Review
Editor reviews drafts for brand, tone, accuracy, and strategic alignment
Augment Keep Human Provisional
+
Hidden judgment layer

What actually happens here

  • The editor applies different treatment by content type — blog, customer email, and social each get a different level of review — but the criteria for each are not documented
  • A style guide exists but doesn't cover everything; "sounds right for the brand" is the operative standard
  • The editor tracks what the exec team cares about this week — strategic messaging priorities that change and aren't formally communicated down
  • The comms lead reviews sensitive pieces, though what qualifies as "sensitive" is also undocumented

Where it breaks down

  • Drafts get sent back multiple times with feedback that isn't always specific enough to act on
  • Output consistency depends on who's reviewing — the editor's judgment isn't systematically the same across all content types
  • During launches, review volume exceeds capacity — the editor spends 20–25 hours per week on review alone during normal weeks, and more during spikes
  • No documented quality standard exists for AI to check against, and the criteria that do exist live in the editor's and comms lead's heads

This classification is provisional — see gap report for what's missing.

Augment
Pre-review triage: before the editor opens a draft, AI checks it against the existing style guide and flags structural issues — grammar, prohibited language, missing CTAs, overly long sentences, format problems by content type. This handles the mechanical layer of review and surfaces specific issues with locations, so the editor's attention can go to judgment rather than hunting. A style guide already exists; AI can operationalize it immediately, even before deeper criteria are documented.
⚠ Watch for
Context drift: the style guide AI checks against will gradually become outdated if it isn't owned and maintained. Assign a specific owner and a review cadence — otherwise AI will confidently flag things the team stopped caring about and miss things they started caring about. Also watch for anchoring: if AI flags a list of issues before the editor reads the draft, the editor's judgment may be unconsciously shaped by what AI found first. Consider having editors note their preliminary read before reviewing the AI triage output.
Keep Human
Final editorial judgment: brand voice beyond what's in the style guide, exec-priority alignment, tone calibration by audience and moment, and the call on what goes back vs. what goes through. This judgment layer is real — the editor can identify what's wrong but often can't fully articulate the criteria in advance. Until the tacit standards that drive these calls are externalized and documented, AI cannot reliably assist with them. The risk of a plausible-but-wrong AI editorial call reaching the approval queue is too high given the downstream consequences.
⚠ Watch for
Key-person dependency: the editorial judgment that makes this step work lives primarily with the editor and comms lead. If either is unavailable during a launch week, the workflow stalls. The judgment isn't documented, can't be delegated, and can't be backed up. Even if AI augmentation doesn't expand here, the team should invest in knowledge codification — not to automate, but so the judgment isn't fragile.
03
Revision & Resubmission
Author addresses editorial feedback and resubmits for another review pass
Augment Stop / Rethink
+
Hidden judgment layer

What actually happens here

  • The author receives tracked changes and/or comments, makes the requested changes, and resubmits
  • Some writers already use ChatGPT to help rewrite sections — pasting the editorial feedback and original text and asking for a revised version
  • No one tracks when AI-assisted revision is happening, and there's no quality check specific to AI-revised content
  • The judgment on whether the revision addressed the feedback lives with the editor, not the author — the author's job here is largely procedural

Where it breaks down

  • Revision cycles multiply: the author makes changes, the editor finds new issues, and the comms lead sometimes introduces new feedback in round 2 or 3 that could have been given in round 1
  • No agreed limit on revision rounds — cycles can extend indefinitely
  • Vague editorial feedback leads to revisions that miss the point, requiring another round
  • Late-entry reviewers (comms lead appearing for the first time in round 3) restart the cycle after the author thought they were close
Augment
Author-side revision assistance: AI helps authors interpret editorial feedback and rewrite specific sections. This is already happening informally — the opportunity is to formalize and guardrail it rather than let it run untracked. A structured revision prompt (original text + specific feedback + brand context) produces more targeted revisions than ad-hoc ChatGPT use. The editor then reviews the revised version against the original feedback, not against general judgment — which speeds re-review.
In use — needs guardrails
⚠ Watch for
Missing review layer: AI-revised content is currently being resubmitted without anyone checking whether the revision was done well or whether it introduced new problems. The editor reviews the final version, but doesn't know which sections came from AI. Consider a lightweight disclosure practice — "AI-assisted sections marked" — so the editor can calibrate review depth appropriately. Also watch for quality erosion: if AI revisions routinely pass review, the standard may quietly lower over time because the work "looks right."
Stop / Rethink
The unlimited revision cycle: there is no agreed maximum number of review rounds, no requirement that all stakeholders give feedback in round 1, and no structured feedback format that specifies what must change vs. what's optional. This is not an AI opportunity — it's a process design problem. Until a revision protocol exists (stakeholder input upfront, clear feedback format, a round limit with an escalation path), adding AI to revision will only make broken cycles faster. Fix the process; then apply AI to the author-side assistance within it.
⚠ Watch for
Automation trap rule applies: the unlimited revision cycle is the broken sub-element here. Do not apply automation or AI-assisted tooling to the revision loop itself (e.g., auto-resubmission triggers, revision tracking dashboards) until the cycle has stopping criteria. Automating around a broken loop scales the problem.
04
Approval
Reviewed draft cleared for publication by the appropriate approver(s)
Stop / Rethink Conditional
+
Hidden judgment layer

What actually happens here

  • The editor decides the draft is ready and routes it for approval — but the routing rules are undocumented
  • Different content types require different approvers (comms lead, director, legal), but the criteria for which content goes where aren't written down anywhere
  • What qualifies as "legally sensitive" for a legal review is undefined — the determination is made case-by-case based on who happens to be reviewing
  • The same content type can receive different scrutiny depending on who's approving and what week it is

Where it breaks down

  • Content sits waiting for approval because it's not clear whose inbox it should be in
  • Approval delays are the number one complaint from content creators — the most painful step in the entire workflow
  • Multiple approvers are sometimes involved without clear sequencing, so content bounces between reviewers
  • The error cost here is potentially high: approved content that shouldn't have been (legal risk, off-brand, factually wrong) creates reputational and compliance exposure
Conditional: The Augment opportunity at this step depends entirely on whether documented approval criteria exist. If the comms team defines an approval decision matrix — which content types require which approvers, what triggers legal review, what constitutes "director-level" content — AI can pre-check content against those criteria before routing and flag likely approval issues in advance. Until those criteria are documented, AI assistance here would produce plausible-but-ungrounded recommendations. The current state is Stop / Rethink. The conditional classification becomes actionable only after that redesign work is done.
Stop / Rethink
The undocumented approval routing: who approves what content, what triggers legal review, and whether the comms lead or director is needed — none of this is written down. The result is inconsistent scrutiny, approval delays, and a step that should be a checklist running as a judgment call every time. This needs to be redesigned into a documented decision matrix before any AI is applied here. Automating undocumented routing rules would encode the current inconsistency into the system and make it harder to correct.
⚠ Watch for
Hidden dependencies: before redesigning the approval step, confirm with legal and leadership what compliance requirements are implicitly being met by the current ad-hoc review practice. The inconsistency feels like a problem from the inside, but the informal legal check may be catching real risks. Stopping or systematizing it without understanding what it currently catches could create compliance gaps that weren't visible before.
Augment — conditional
Once approval criteria are documented: AI pre-checks content against the approval decision matrix before routing — flags content likely to require legal review, identifies off-brand claims the director should see, and routes automatically to the correct approver. This compresses the most painful step in the workflow from "figure out whose job this is" to "confirm the AI routing is correct." But this is a downstream opportunity. The Stop / Rethink work comes first.
⚠ Watch for
Plausible-but-wrong: an AI that confidently routes content to the wrong approver is worse than the current state — it creates false certainty. The AI routing recommendation must be treated as a draft for human confirmation, not a final decision, until the routing criteria have been tested and validated. Do not remove the human confirmation step from approval routing, even after criteria are documented.
05
Publication
Approved content published to target channels (blog, social, email, partner portal)
Automate Augment Keep Human
+
Hidden judgment layer

What actually happens here

  • Approved content is manually reformatted for each channel — copy-pasting between platforms and adjusting for character limits, image specs, and CTA placement
  • The social media manager uses an AI feature in the scheduling tool to suggest optimal posting times; she overrides it ~20% of the time based on her own sense of when the audience is active
  • There's a channel formatting checklist, though it's slightly out of date
  • The social media manager's knowledge of optimal posting times is accurate but entirely undocumented

Where it breaks down

  • Multi-channel reformatting is tedious and repetitive — the same content gets manually adjusted for each platform every time
  • Occasionally the wrong version gets published (pre-approval draft instead of final) — a versioning issue, not a judgment issue
  • Formatting errors occur when content moves between platforms; the checklist is slightly outdated
  • Publication is otherwise the smoothest step: once content is approved, it reliably gets published
Automate
Multi-channel reformatting: from approved content, automatically generate channel-specific versions — social post within character limits, email subject line and preheader, blog meta description, partner portal format. The rules for each channel are documented (if slightly outdated — update the checklist first). This eliminates the copy-paste cycle and reduces the formatting error rate. The social media manager reviews before scheduling; the automation handles the mechanical adaptation.
⚠ Watch for
Brittleness: the reformatting automation depends on the channel specification checklist being current. If the checklist is outdated and the automation is built from it, the automation will confidently produce incorrectly formatted content. Update the checklist before building the automation — not after.
Augment
AI-assisted posting time optimization in the scheduling tool — already in use and working. The social media manager has calibrated the AI's recommendations against her own audience knowledge, maintains active override judgment (~20% override rate), and uses the tool as an input rather than a final answer. This is a functional human-AI collaboration pattern. No changes recommended; continue and monitor for drift toward over-reliance as the tool is used longer.
In use — working
Keep Human
Final pre-publish review: confirming the correct version (approved, not draft), the correct channel, and that formatting is right before content goes live. This is the last error-detection checkpoint before external audiences see the content. Given that wrong-version publication has happened before, and given the reputational cost of a bad post at scale, the human review gate at publication should be maintained even after reformatting is automated.

Skills to Act on This Map
Capabilities needed to execute
Knowledge Codification
The highest-leverage work before any AI can be applied to Steps 2 and 4. The editorial quality criteria, content-type review standards, and approval routing rules currently live in the editor's and comms lead's heads. Externalizing these — into a documented style expansion, a review rubric by content type, and a written approval decision matrix — is the precondition for augmentation. This is not a technology skill; it's a structured documentation process.
Process Design
Three sub-elements are Stop / Rethink: the informal urgency queue-jump (Step 1), the unlimited revision cycle with late-entry reviewers (Step 3), and the undocumented approval routing (Step 4). These are process design problems, not AI problems. Each needs a redesigned protocol — an urgency tier in the intake form, a revision protocol with a round limit and front-loaded stakeholder input, and an approval decision matrix — before any AI opportunity in those areas becomes viable.
Workflow Automation Design
Intake validation and queue entry (Step 1) and multi-channel reformatting (Step 5) are ready to automate once the intake channel is consolidated and the channel checklist is updated. Both require mapping triggers, conditions, and actions across the tools already in use (Google Drive, scheduling tools, email platforms). The scope is well-defined — this is integration work more than AI work.
Prompt Engineering
The pre-review triage in Step 2 and the author revision assistance in Step 3 both require prompts that are grounded in the documented standards that knowledge codification produces. Generic AI prompts won't work here — the prompts need to reference the specific style guide, content-type criteria, and feedback format the team defines. Prompt engineering in this context means building reliable assistants that check against the team's own standards, not general writing best practices.
Quality Evaluation Design
The Step 2 Augment classification is Provisional specifically because no quality standard exists for AI to check against. Defining what "good" looks like per content type — in terms specific enough for AI to evaluate — is the prerequisite for meaningful augmentation in editorial review. This skill bridges knowledge codification (surfacing the criteria) and prompt engineering (building AI to apply them).
One step (Step 2: Editorial Review) carries a Provisional classification. A gap report has been produced separately: content-approval-gap-report.md. Three sub-elements across Steps 1, 3, and 4 are classified Stop / Rethink. Per the automation trap rule, these sub-elements do not receive Automate classifications — automating a broken or undefined process scales the problem. The Stop / Rethink work at Steps 3 and 4 is foundational: revision cycles and approval routing are the two biggest bottlenecks in this workflow, and AI opportunity in both areas is conditional on that redesign happening first.
Recommended next audits
Editorial Review (Step 2) — sub-workflow audit. This step is currently Provisional because the judgment layer is thin: criteria for different content types aren't documented, what triggers a comms lead review isn't defined, and what makes feedback actionable vs. vague isn't captured. A sub-workflow audit focused on the editor's actual decision process — what they check first, what they check differently by content type, what they look for when the comms lead needs to see something — would produce a more precise Augment scope and unlock the knowledge codification work this map depends on.
Approval routing (Step 4) — decision matrix design session. The approval step cannot be classified beyond Stop / Rethink until someone maps what the approval criteria actually are. A structured session with the comms lead, director, and legal to surface the implicit routing rules — what content types require which approvers, what triggers legal, what constitutes director-level sensitivity — would convert a Conditional into a Confident classification and unlock the most impactful AI opportunity in this workflow.
