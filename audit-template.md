# Workflow Audit Template

Fill this out for the workflow you want to map. Be specific and honest. The operator produces better output when the input is candid about what's messy, unclear, or broken. There are no wrong answers, but vague answers produce vague maps.

Complete the workflow-level section once at the top, then copy the step template for each step in your workflow. Most workflows have 4-8 steps. If yours has more than 10, consider whether you're actually looking at two workflows.

---

## Workflow-level framing

**Workflow name:**

> *Give it a plain name. "Content approval process" not "Phase 2 cross-functional content lifecycle."*

**Outcome: what is this workflow supposed to produce or enable?**

> *This is the most important question in the audit. State what changes in the world when this workflow runs well. A specific outcome sounds like: "Produce learning assets that measurably close a defined knowledge gap for a specific audience." A vague outcome sounds like: "Keep stakeholders informed" or "Support the team." If you struggle to answer this, that's a signal worth paying attention to.*

**How often does it run?**

> *Frequency and volume. "8-12 projects per quarter" or "30-50 pieces per week" or "once per sprint." This helps the operator gauge the impact of improvements.*

**Who owns it?**

> *The team or person accountable for the workflow. Not who does the work, but who would answer for it if asked.*

**What does "good" look like? Describe this workflow running perfectly.**

> *Paint the picture. What happens when everything goes right? How fast does it move? What does the output look like? What doesn't happen (no rework, no confusion, no delays)? This becomes the measurement baseline. If you can't describe "good," the operator will flag that.*

---

## Workflow value

These questions help determine whether this workflow is worth investing in. Not every workflow needs AI redesign. These answers help prioritize.

**What happens if this workflow breaks down or runs badly?**

> *Think about consequences, not just inconvenience. Does it affect customers? Revenue? Employee experience? Compliance? Decision quality? If this workflow failed for a week, who would notice and what would go wrong? A high-impact answer sounds like: "Customer onboarding stalls, time-to-value increases, and churn risk goes up." A low-impact answer sounds like: "A report would be late." Both are honest. The distinction matters for prioritization.*

**How many people, teams, or customers are affected by this workflow's output?**

> *Scale matters. A workflow that serves 5 people is a different investment than one that serves 5,000. Count the people who use, receive, or depend on what this workflow produces — not just the people who do the work.*

**Roughly how much time does this workflow consume per cycle, across everyone involved?**

> *Add it up across all roles. If 6 people each spend 2 hours per week, that's 12 person-hours per week. This helps quantify the cost of the current state and the potential value of improvement. Estimates are fine.*

**Is this workflow connected to something leadership is actively trying to improve?**

> *Strategic relevance. Is there executive attention on this area? Is it tied to a current initiative, OKR, or known pain point at the leadership level? This isn't required, but it affects prioritization and support for redesign.*

**If AI made this workflow significantly better, would the people doing the work feel it?**

> *This is about delight potential. Would the improvement free up meaningful time? Reduce real frustration? Enable work people actually want to do? Or would it shave a few minutes off something that's already fine? The strongest AI transformation wins happen when people feel a real difference in their day-to-day — not just when a metric improves.*

## Step template

### Step [number]: [Step name]

**What triggers this step?**

> *What has to happen before this step starts? A request arrives, a draft is complete, a meeting ends, an approval is given. Name the trigger.*

**What does this step produce?**

> *What exists after this step that didn't exist before? A decision, a document, a draft, a set of findings, an approval. If the output is hard to name, that's worth noting.*

#### Judgment layer

These four questions surface the hidden knowledge that experienced people carry. They're the most important part of the audit. Take your time with them.

**What would a new person get wrong here that an experienced person wouldn't?**

> *This is the question that reveals tacit expertise. Think about the last time you onboarded someone. What did they struggle with that you now do without thinking? What shortcuts do you take that you'd never put in a training manual? What mistakes did you make early on that you've learned to avoid?*
>
> *A good answer sounds like: "They would accept the request as stated. Experienced people know the stated need is often not the real need. The first call is whether training is even the right intervention."*
>
> *A thin answer sounds like: "They'd need time to learn the process." That's true of any step. What specifically would they get wrong?*

**What information do you use here that isn't written down anywhere?**

> *The stuff in your head. Stakeholder history, pattern recognition from past projects, knowledge of who to call, political dynamics, institutional memory. If you disappeared tomorrow, what would your replacement not be able to find in any document, wiki, or system?*
>
> *A good answer sounds like: "Who is asking matters. Some stakeholders have a track record of clear requests. Others consistently change direction mid-project. I adjust my intake process based on who's across the table."*
>
> *A thin answer sounds like: "Nothing, it's all documented." If that's genuinely true, great. But for most knowledge work, there's more in your head than in the system.*

**When this step goes badly, what usually caused it?**

> *Think about the last few times this step failed or produced a bad result. What happened? Don't describe what went wrong at the surface level. Describe what caused it. "The client changed their mind" is a surface description. "We didn't ask enough diagnostic questions during intake, so we built to the stated need instead of the real need" is a root cause.*
>
> *A good answer names a specific, recurring pattern. A thin answer says "things go wrong sometimes" or "it depends on the situation."*

**Who else is involved in this step, and what do you need from them?**

> *Name the other people or roles. What do you need from them for this step to work? What happens when they don't deliver? This surfaces handoffs, dependencies, and places where the workflow depends on relationships rather than process.*
>
> *If this step depends on getting something from someone who's unreliable, say so. The operator needs to know where the workflow is fragile.*

#### Classification signals

These questions help the operator determine what kind of AI opportunity exists.

**Can someone follow a checklist for this step, or does it require judgment?**

> *Be honest about which parts are procedural and which parts require thinking. Many steps are split: the mechanics are checklistable but the decisions within them aren't. If it's a split, say so and describe both parts.*
>
> *"Follow a checklist" means: given the same input, two different people would produce the same output by following instructions. "Requires judgment" means: the output depends on the person's experience, taste, or read of the situation.*

**How consistently does this step produce a reliable output?**

> *If three different people ran this step on the same input, would they produce roughly the same result? Or would the outputs vary significantly? High variance usually means undocumented judgment. Low variance usually means the step is more procedural than it seems.*

**What's the cost if this step is done wrong?**

> *Think about severity and reversibility. A typo in an email is low-cost and reversible. A misdiagnosed need that leads to building the wrong thing is high-cost and hard to reverse. Where does this step fall?*

**Does this step currently slow the workflow down?**

> *Is this step a bottleneck? If so, is it slow because of volume (too many things to process), complexity (each one takes a long time to think through), or dependency (waiting on someone else)?*

**What parts of this step are the most tedious, time-consuming, or unpleasant?**

> *Point at the friction. What drains you? What do you dread? What would you hand off to someone (or something) else if you could?*
>
> *This question matters because different kinds of friction point to different AI opportunities:*
> *- "It's tedious and repetitive" suggests automation potential.*
> *- "It's cognitively heavy, too much to hold in my head" suggests augmentation potential.*
> *- "It's painful and I'm not sure why we do it" suggests the step might need rethinking.*
> *- "It's hard but it's where I add the most value" suggests it should stay human.*
>
> *All of these are useful answers. There's no wrong kind of friction.*

#### Current AI usage

**Are you currently using AI in this step? If so, describe what you use, what it does, and how you check the output.**

> *If you're using ChatGPT, Copilot, a built-in AI feature, or any other AI tool as part of how you do this step, describe it here. What tool? What do you use it for (drafting, summarizing, analyzing, generating, checking)? How do you review what it produces — do you read it carefully, skim it, or mostly trust it?*
>
> *If you're not using AI here, just say so. That's useful information too.*
>
> *A good answer sounds like: "I use ChatGPT to draft the first version of the brief. I give it the project details and ask for a structured draft. I usually edit about 30% of what it gives me — it gets the structure right but misses our voice. I don't have a formal review checklist, I just read it and fix what feels off."*
>
> *A thin answer sounds like: "Yes, I use AI sometimes." What tool? For what? How do you check it?*
